Lecture 1 — What is Law?

The starting idea of the whole course is that law is a multidimensional phenomenon (un fenómeno multidimensional): you cannot capture it with one clean definition. Hart, opening The Concept of Law, notes that no other discipline keeps re-asking its own basic question the way law does — "What is law?" has been answered "in diverse, strange and even paradoxical ways". Nobody writes libraries on "what is chemistry?". Bobbio adds that every definition of law carries "an element of arbitrariness" (un elemento de arbitrariedad), because you must decide where to draw the boundary of what counts as "law".

Because of this, the lecture looks at law from three angles at once. They are not rival definitions — they are three faces of the same thing.

The three dimensions

DimensionWhat it means (explained)
Law as a human phenomenon (hecho social)Law is not abstract: it grows out of concrete social realities — the context, ideology and conflicts of a society. This is its historical character (carácter histórico): law changes over time as society changes. What is lawful today may be unlawful tomorrow.
Law as societal ordering (ordenación social)Law sets patterns and models of behaviour. It steers conduct — sometimes gently (think of nudge theory (teoría del "empujón"), like a smoking ban). Cicero captures the paradox: "We are slaves of the law so that we may be free" — rules constrain us, yet they make ordered freedom possible.
Law as a system of values (sistema de valores)Every legal order embodies a view of justice and moral rightness. The key exam line: no legal system is politically neutral — the values behind the rules reflect particular interests and beliefs.

The two great functions — Lord Denning

"The law has two great objects: to preserve order and to do justice; and the two do not always coincide."

This tension is the heart of the lecture. Sometimes keeping order (a clear, predictable rule) means not doing perfect justice in the individual case, and vice versa.

  • Order (orden/seguridad): without law there is no order. Its core is legal certainty (seguridad jurídica), which has three parts — certainty about (1) the content of the law, (2) that it will be enforced (aplicada coactivamente), and (3) that it will be applied consistently.
  • Justice: aims at balance and proportion — restoring equilibrium. Two classic maxims: treat like cases alike (tratar igual lo igual) and treat different cases differently (tratar distinto lo distinto).

Key case — Bellinger v Bellinger (2001/2003)

Mrs Bellinger, a transsexual woman, asked the court to recognise her marriage as valid. The majority held that the biological factors from Corbett v Corbett (chromosomal, gonadal and genital) were determinative, and — crucially — that changing the legal definition of "female" was a matter for Parliament, not the courts, because it is a big question of public policy (política legislativa). The dissenting judge thought "the world had moved on" scientifically and socially. The case is the perfect illustration of order/legal certainty vs justice: the court chose the certain, established rule over an individually fairer outcome, leaving change to the legislator.

🔑 Exam essentials
  • 3 dimensions: fact / system of norms / system of values.
  • Denning: order vs justice (they don't always coincide).
  • Legal certainty = content + enforcement + consistency.
  • Bellinger/Corbett: defining "female" is for Parliament, not courts.
⚠️ Watch out"No legal system is politically neutral" is TRUE. Don't confuse order (predictability) with justice (fairness).

Lecture 2 — A Multiplicity of Legal Systems

If law is a product of society, then different societies produce different legal traditions and cultures. A "legal family" (familia jurídica) is a group of legal systems that share the same broad style, sources and history. These families are living, evolving tools — not fixed boxes.

Western legal tradition — its 4 defining features

  • Clear demarcation (delimitación clara) between legal institutions and other kinds (religious, political).
  • Rule of law (imperio de la ley) — legal authority is supreme over political power. This is the most important feature.
  • Legal doctrine (doctrina jurídica) as the principal source and the basis of legal practice.
  • Coherent body — law is treated as a coherent whole with its own internal logic.
Rule of law: the idea that "no one is above the law" — a rational system of rules that are general, clear, prospective (que miran al futuro, no retroactivas) and impartial. It cannot guarantee freedom, but it is an indispensable curb on the abuse of power, securing fairness, transparency and equal access to justice.

The legal families

FamilyExplanation & examples
Civil law (derecho continental, codificado)Built on written codes. Sub-groups: French (France, Belgium, Spain, Italy, ex-colonies), German (Germany, Austria, Switzerland, Japan, S. Korea, Taiwan), Scandinavian, and Chinese (civil law + socialist law).
Common law (derecho anglosajón)Essentially unwritten / non-textual: the building blocks are cases, not codes, governed by the doctrine of precedent (la regla del precedente: lo decidido antes vincula).
Mixed / hybrid (sistemas mixtos)Combine traditions — e.g. South Africa = Roman-Dutch law + English common law.

Divisions of law

  • Public vs Private: public law is where the State is a party/plays a role (constitutional, administrative, criminal); private law regulates relations between individuals where the State does not (contract, property, tort (responsabilidad civil extracontractual)). EU law blurs this line.
  • Substantive vs Procedural: substantive law (derecho sustantivo) gives people rights and tells them what to do; procedural law (derecho procesal) provides the rules for court procedure and the organisation of the judiciary.
🔑 Exam essentials
  • 4 features of the Western tradition; rule of law is the key one.
  • Civil vs common vs mixed; public/private; substantive/procedural.
⚠️ Watch outJapan is German civil law, not French. "Common law = unwritten" is still true conceptually, even though modern common law is heavily statutory.

Lecture 3 — Exploring Normativity

This lecture zooms into the second dimension: law as a normative system (sistema normativo). "Normative" means it does not describe the world — it prescribes (prescribe: ordena cómo debe ser) conduct. Every legal norm classifies behaviour as compulsory, prohibited or allowed (obligatorio, prohibido o permitido). Law therefore belongs to the world of "what ought to be" ("el deber ser"), not "what is".

Law builds these behavioural models through language, and language comes in two types you must be able to tell apart:

  • Descriptive statements (enunciados descriptivos) → describe reality ("what is"). They can be analysed as true or false.
  • Prescriptive statements (enunciados prescriptivos) → prescribe human conduct ("what ought to be"). They cannot be true or false — only obeyed or disobeyed, just or unjust.

The classic exercise is to classify constitutional/legal articles. "The capital of Spain is Madrid" looks like a rule but is really descriptive; "Non-emancipated minors cannot marry" is prescriptive. The point: because legal norms are prescriptive, we cannot judge them as true/false — we need different tools (validity, efficacy, justice — see Lecture 4).

🔑 Exam essentials
  • Norms classify conduct as compulsory / prohibited / allowed.
  • Descriptive (true/false, "what is") vs prescriptive (not true/false, "what ought to be").

Lecture 4 — Normative Dimension II: Validity / Efficacy / Justice

Since we cannot call a norm "true" or "false", how do we analyse it? With three criteria — the pillar of the whole course. Learn to define each one precisely.

CriterionWhat it assesses (explained)
Validity (validez)A relational concept: a norm is valid because it belongs to a particular legal system — i.e. it relates correctly to the other rules of that system. Validity means the norm is binding (vinculante) and backed by the State's enforcement machinery (aparato coactivo del Estado). It requires both formal requirements (right authority, right procedure) and material requirements (content not contradicting superior rules).
Efficacy (eficacia)Is the norm actually obeyed in practice? It looks at obedience and enforcement (people are punished when they break it). It is about the norm's real impact on behaviour.
Justice (justicia)Does the norm fit an ideal system of values? This is the axiological dimension (dimensión axiológica: relativa a los valores), traditionally seen as the ultimate goal of law.

Why do people obey the law? Four reasons: habit (they don't even notice it's law), avoiding punishment, agreement (they share the norm's aim), and self-interest.

The compliance spectrum (espectro de cumplimiento) runs from most to least obeyed: spontaneous compliance (everyone obeys, no coercion) → threat-induced compliance (only obeyed under threat of sanction) → non-compliance despite threat → complete non-compliance.

⚠️ Watch out — favourite trapEfficacy (eficacia) = is it obeyed / punished?  ≠  Efficiency (eficiencia) = did the norm achieve its intended goal? A norm can be efficacious but not efficient.
🔑 Exam essentials
  • Validity = relational + binding + (formal & material requirements).
  • Efficacy = obedience/enforcement; Justice = fit with ideal values (axiological).
  • Efficacy ≠ efficiency; 4 reasons to obey; compliance spectrum.

Lecture 5 — Normative Dimension III: Conceptual Independence

This short lecture asks: are validity, efficacy and justice connected? The answer depends on the level you look at.

  • At the level of a single norm → conceptual independence (independencia conceptual): the three are logically independent. That is why all 8 combinations are possible — a norm can be valid–ineffective–unjust, valid–effective–just, invalid–effective–just, and so on. (2 × 2 × 2 = 8.) Knowing a norm is valid tells you nothing automatic about whether it is obeyed or just.
  • At the level of the legal system → conceptual interdependence (interdependencia conceptual):
    • Validity & effectiveness: a whole system must be broadly effective to exist at all — a system nobody obeys is not really in force.
    • Validity & justice: democratic systems positivise (positivizan: incorporan al derecho escrito) ideas of justice in their superior norms (e.g. Art. 1.1 of the Spanish Constitution, Title I). Since a condition of validity is not contradicting the superior norms, a norm — to be valid — cannot contradict those positivised contents of justice. So at system level, validity and justice become linked.
🔑 Exam essentials
  • Norm level = independence (the 8 combinations); system level = interdependence.
  • Validity & justice link up because the constitution positivises justice.

Lecture 8 — Legal Realism

Legal realism is the third great theory of law (after natural law and positivism). Its bold claim: law is simply the standards actually enforced by the courts. In the validity/efficacy/justice scheme, realism reduces validity to efficacy — a norm is "valid" just in case judges in fact apply it. Forget books and abstract principles; look at what courts really do.

American RealismScandinavian Realism
Pragmatist and behaviourist; studies "law in action" (el derecho en acción); immersed in the judicial process.A philosophical attack on the metaphysical (metafísicos: abstractos, no empíricos) foundations of law; concerned with the whole system.

Both schools reject mixing law with morality and distrust absolute values like "justice".

Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr: "The prophecies of what the courts will do in fact… are what I mean by the law" — this is law as prophecy (el derecho como profecía/predicción). Also: "The life of the law has not been logic: it has been experience."

Holmes's idea is practical: a lawyer advising a client really wants to predict what a court will do. So "the law" just is a prediction about the courts' future use of public force.

  • Judges' decisions often rest on hunches (corazonadas) and personal or political bias; legal rules are indeterminate (indeterminadas: no dan una única respuesta) and rarely neutral; the social sciences should play a bigger role.
  • Realism reacts against legal formalism / mechanical jurisprudence (formalismo: deducir la sentencia de reglas como una máquina). Case: US v E.C. Knight Co (the Sugar Trust case) — a monopoly controlling 98% of sugar refining escaped regulation because the court decided "on labels" (manufacturing ≠ interstate commerce), ignoring obvious real-world effects.
  • Hungry judge effect (el "efecto del juez hambriento"): studies show judges rule more leniently after meal breaks — a vivid proof that non-legal factors shape decisions.
  • Scandinavian realism (Alf Ross): legal norms are directives addressed to judges; legal science is an empirical science — saying "norm N is the law in force in country P" is really a prediction that judges will apply N. Influenced by logical empiricism and falsifiability (falsabilidad: que pueda refutarse) — see McLean v Arkansas: "creation science" is not falsifiable, so it is not science.
🔑 Exam essentials
  • Validity → efficacy; law = what courts actually do (prediction).
  • Holmes: law as prophecy; "experience, not logic".
  • American vs Scandinavian (Alf Ross); Sugar Trust; hungry judge; McLean v Arkansas (falsifiability).

Lecture 9 — Justice & Legal Systems

Now we open the third dimension properly: law as values. Justice is a moral concept — a subset of morality (un subconjunto de la moral) that only makes sense where there is consciousness, rationality and a moral sense.

Alf Ross (the sceptic): "To invoke justice is the same thing as banging on the table (dar un golpe en la mesa): an emotional expression which turns one's demand into an absolute postulate." — i.e. shouting "justice!" ends rational argument rather than advancing it.

We can call three things just or unjust: agents (a just person), actions (a just decision) and states of affairs (situaciones/estados de cosas) (a just legal system). Note: suffering caused by a natural disaster is not in itself an injustice — injustice arises from failing to relieve it, or helping some at the expense of others.

The three types of justice

TypeExplanation
Corrective justice (justicia correctiva)Puts right a wrong between two parties — restoring the equilibrium that was tilted. Typical of tort and contract law. Symbolised by the scales (la balanza) and the blindfold (la venda) of the statue of Justice (balance + impartiality).
Distributive justice (justicia distributiva)The fair allocation of goods in a group — "giving each person their due". In democracies it rests on three values: freedom, equality, solidarity. Chiefly the concern of the legislator.
Formal justice (justicia formal)Legal certainty: the consistent application of rules. Its core is depersonalisation (despersonalización) — rules apply to the act, not the actor (al acto, no a la persona), the opposite of the primitive instinct to judge the character of the parties.

Where formal justice shows up: against power (habeas corpus (garantía contra la detención ilegal), in dubio pro reo (en caso de duda, a favor del reo)); inside the system (validity criteria as a mechanism of certainty — back to Fuller); in society (binding private contracts, respect for property).

🔑 Exam essentials
  • Justice = moral concept; Ross "banging on the table".
  • Just/unjust of agents, actions, states of affairs.
  • Corrective / distributive (freedom-equality-solidarity) / formal (act not actor; legal certainty).

Lecture 10 — Theories of Justice

If justice matters, what does a just society look like? Three big theories give very different answers.

1) Utilitarianism (utilitarismo)

Bentham: nature has placed us under two "sovereign masters", pain and pleasure; the principle of utility (principio de utilidad) says we should maximise overall happiness (modern version: satisfy people's preferences). Two versions:

  • Act utilitarianism: judge each action by its consequences.
  • Rule utilitarianism: judge the rule that everyone should follow in similar cases — more relevant in law, since we usually assess the justice of a rule.

The transplant dilemma (el dilema del trasplante): a doctor could kill 1 healthy person to save 5 patients. Act utilitarianism seems to say yes (5 > 1); rule utilitarianism says no — a rule "doctors may kill the healthy" would destroy trust in medicine. Critiques: devaluation of personhood (people reduced to mere "experiencers" of pleasure), the distribution problem (it ignores how happiness is shared out), and majoritarianism (mayoritarismo: la mayoría puede sacrificar a la minoría).

2) Rawls — Justice as Fairness (la justicia como equidad)

Rawls wanted a systematic alternative to utilitarianism, which he thought could not protect basic rights. His device is the veil of ignorance (velo de ignorancia): imagine designing society's rules without knowing your own position in it (your gender, wealth, race, talents). Choosing from behind the veil is impartial, so the principles chosen are legitimate. Rational people would follow the maximin (criterio maximin: maximizar el mínimo) rule — pick the arrangement with the best worst-case. This yields two principles: (1) equal basic liberties for all; (2) democratic equality of opportunity, reducing the arbitrary advantages of the "natural lottery" (la lotería natural: con qué talentos/familia naces) (e.g. via education).

3) Nozick — Libertarianism (libertarismo)

Nozick defends a minimal state (Estado mínimo) and says distributive justice is irrelevant. His entitlement theory (teoría de la titularidad/los títulos): a distribution is just if it was reached through voluntary exchange or gift — there is "no central distribution". For him liberty and equality are incompatible: keeping everyone equal would require constantly interfering with people's free choices.

🔑 Exam essentials
  • Act vs rule utilitarianism + transplant dilemma + 3 critiques.
  • Rawls: veil of ignorance, maximin, two principles.
  • Nozick: minimal state, entitlement theory, liberty vs equality.

Lecture 11 — The Civil Law Tradition: Roman Law

The civil law tradition is the world's oldest and most widespread legal system. Its deep foundation rests on three pillars: Roman law, Germanic customary law (derecho consuetudinario germánico) and Canon law (derecho canónico: el de la Iglesia). Roman law is the dominant pillar, transmitted through Justinian's 6th-century codification.

The evolution of Roman law runs through four periods: Archaic → Pre-classical → Classical (the Principate (el Principado: el primer Imperio)) → Post-classical. Key milestones:

  • Twelve Tables (450 BC) (Ley de las XII Tablas) — the first comprehensive codification of Roman civil law, made public so citizens knew their rights; it remained the only such code for ~1000 years.
  • Lex Hortensia (287 BC) made plebiscites (decisiones de la plebe) binding on all citizens; the Praetor Peregrinus (242 BC) (pretor para extranjeros) handled disputes with foreigners, giving rise to the ius gentium.
  • Constitutio Antoniniana (AD 212) extended citizenship to all free inhabitants — after which the civile/gentium distinction effectively vanished.
Body of lawMeaning
Ius civile (derecho civil/de los ciudadanos)The law of Roman citizens — the principle of personality of law (el derecho se aplica por la persona, no por el territorio).
Ius gentium (derecho de gentes)Institutions and principles common to all peoples under Rome, regardless of citizenship — it widened the scope of the law.
Ius honorarium (derecho honorario/pretorio)Law created by the magistrates (especially the praetors (pretores)) when applying the law — flexible, progressive, ever-developing.

Corpus Juris Civilis (Cuerpo de Derecho Civil), completed under Justinian (534), has three parts: the Codex (imperial legislation), the Digest (el Digesto: la parte mayor, extractos de juristas), and the Institutions (manual para estudiantes). Substantively, Roman jurists split ius privatum (derecho privado) into the law of persons, of things and of actions (personas, cosas y acciones procesales) — and it was the most creative part of Roman legal life.

🔑 Exam essentials
  • 3 pillars (Roman/Germanic/Canon); Twelve Tables 450 BC.
  • ius civile / gentium / honorarium; Corpus Juris Civilis (Codex/Digest/Institutions).
  • ius privatum = persons / things / actions.

Lecture 12 — Ius Commune & the Codification of Law

How did Roman law travel from antiquity into modern European codes? Through the ius commune and then the codification movement.

  • 476 AD: the fall of the Western Roman Empire caused legal fragmentation (fragmentación jurídica). Roman law survived only in a "vulgarised" (vulgarizada: simplificada/degradada) form, mixed with Germanic custom.
  • Revival (11th–12th c.) (el "renacimiento" del derecho romano): the rediscovery of Justinian's Digest provided a sophisticated, rational system. The political struggle between Empire and Papacy (Imperio y Papado) fed interest in it, and the new universities (Bologna, Paris, Salamanca) trained a professional class of jurists.
  • Ius commune (derecho común europeo) = Roman + Canon + Germanic custom — a shared, universalising legal foundation across Continental Europe (received unevenly by region).
  • Humanists and the School of Natural Law (Escuela del Derecho Natural) (Grotius, Pufendorf, Thomasius, Wolff) supplied the rational, ideological basis for later codification.
CodeFeatures
French Civil Code 1804 (Code Napoléon)Unified French law (Roman + customary + royal + revolutionary). Built on three pillars: private property, freedom of contract, the patriarchal family (la familia patriarcal). Uses broad, flexible rules that leave room for the judge to fill gaps.
German Civil Code / BGB 1900 (el BGB)5 books (general principles, obligations, property, family, succession). Famous for conceptualism (conceptualismo: definiciones técnicas muy precisas). Driven by national unification — and seen as more modern than the 1804 French code.
🔑 Exam essentials
  • 476 → revival (Digest / Bologna) → ius commune = Roman + Canon + Germanic.
  • School of Natural Law as the bridge to codification.
  • French Code 1804 (3 pillars, flexible) vs German BGB 1900 (5 books, conceptualism).

Lecture 14 — The Common Law Tradition: History

The common law is England's home-grown, judge-made system. Two famous critics mocked the idea that judges merely "find" pre-existing law:

Bentham: the common law is "a thing merely imaginary." Austin: a "childish fiction… made by nobody" — attacking the declaratory theory (teoría declarativa: el juez sólo "declara" un derecho que ya existía).
  • Pre-Norman England used Germanic folk-law (derecho popular germánico). The Norman Conquest (1066) began the centralisation that made a single law possible.
  • Henry II (1154–89) divided England into circuits (circuitos judiciales) and regularly sent royal judges around the country; gradually their decisions fused into a law "common" to the whole realm — hence "common law".
  • The writ system: a lawsuit began with a writ (orden/mandato escrito del rey) issued by the chancery. Each writ became a precedent; from the 14th c. a writ would issue only if the facts matched an earlier case (the Register of Writs (Registro de Writs)). No suitable writ → no remedy. The rigid forms of action (las "formas de acción": categorías procesales cerradas) classified and then ossified (anquilosaron) claims — producing rigidity and injustice, and that is exactly what triggered the rise of equity (Lecture 15).
  • 16th-century crisis: there was pressure to "receive" Roman law as on the Continent. The common law survived thanks to four factors: the Tudor monarchs preferred to reshape existing institutions; new courts (Chancery, Star Chamber) covered its gaps; Coke's restatement modernised its principles; and the Inns of Court (los colegios de abogados de Londres) preserved "the toughness of a taught tradition" (Maitland).
🔑 Exam essentials
  • Bentham/Austin attack the declaratory theory.
  • Henry II + circuits → law "common" to all; writ system / forms of action / Register of Writs → rigidity → equity.
  • 16th-c. crisis survived via 4 factors (Tudors, new courts, Coke, Inns of Court).

Lecture 15 — The Rise of Equity

The common law grew out of the king's council (curia regis (la corte/consejo del rey)), but it became rigid. Equity (equity = la "equidad"; un segundo sistema que corrige al common law) developed to fix its shortcomings (deficiencias):

  • Limited writs — if no suitable writ existed, the victim had no remedy.
  • Inflexibility — the rigid rules could produce unconscionable (contrario a la conciencia/injusto) results.
  • Corruption & intimidation — courts could be swayed by the powerful, and the common law had no answer.

Equity = fairness / justice. A wronged person would petition the monarch (elevar una petición al rey) — "the fountain of justice" — appealing to conscience (la conciencia). The king delegated these petitions to the Lord Chancellor (el Lord Canciller), and so the Court of Chancery (el Tribunal de la Cancillería) emerged (late 14th c.). Over time equity was systematised from arbitrary "conscience" into settled principles: Lord Nottingham (1675), Lord Hardwicke (1737), and Lord Eldon (1801, insisting on precedent).

When the two systems clashed, the conflict was settled under James I (Earl of Oxford's Case, 1615): equity prevails (la equity prevalece). The Judicature Acts 1873–75 created one Supreme Court where any division could apply both common law and equity, and confirmed that in conflict equity prevails. The merger debate (el debate sobre la fusión) — did the two systems truly fuse, or just their administration? Ashburner's famous image: "the two streams of jurisdiction… run side by side and do not mingle their waters" (the dominant view: not fused). Equity works through flexible maxims (máximas/principios) — e.g. "he who comes to equity must come with clean hands" ("manos limpias": quien pide equidad debe haber actuado correctamente) — and discretionary equitable remedies: injunction (orden judicial de hacer/no hacer), specific performance (cumplimiento forzoso), rescission, rectification, account.

🔑 Exam essentials
  • 3 shortcomings → petition the monarch → Chancellor → Court of Chancery (conscience).
  • Earl of Oxford's Case (1615) & Judicature Acts 1873–75: equity prevails.
  • Merger debate (Ashburner "two streams"); maxims; clean hands; equitable remedies.

Lecture 19 — Sovereignty: From Westphalia to Globalization ★ Most likely

This is one of your most likely exam topics. The Peace of Westphalia (1648) (la Paz de Westfalia) ended a series of wars and re-organised Europe into sovereign Nation-States, each with exclusive power over its own territory. A major consequence: law became primarily national law (before, law belonged to a people/tribe, not a territory). This created the Westphalian duo (el "dúo de Westfalia") — the only two recognised forms of law: national State law + international public law.

Essential elements of the State: sovereignty (soberanía) · government · territory & population.

Classical theories of sovereignty (know who says what)

AuthorThesis
Jean Bodin (16th c.)Sovereignty = "absolute and perpetual power"; supports a strong monarchy.
Thomas Hobbes (1651)Justifies absolute sovereignty through the social contract (contrato social) — individuals surrender freedom in exchange for security.
John Locke (1689)Limited sovereignty — government derives from the consent of the people (consentimiento del pueblo).
JJ. Rousseau (1762)Popular sovereignty — the people's general will (voluntad general) should guide government.

Types of sovereignty: internal (supreme authority inside the territory) vs external (recognition as independent abroad); legal vs political; negative (right to non-interference — key in decolonisation (descolonización)) vs positive (the real capacity to govern). Modern challenges: globalisation & economic integration, international human rights, and supranational institutions (instituciones supranacionales) (EU, WTO, UN). There was also a shift from natural law to legal positivism as states needed formal, non-religious systems. Constitutional milestones: Magna Carta 1215 → English Bill of Rights 1689 → US Constitution 1787 → French Revolution 1789.

🔑 Exam essentials
  • Westphalia 1648 → sovereign nation-states → national law; the Westphalian duo.
  • Bodin / Hobbes / Locke / Rousseau — match each to his thesis.
  • Types of sovereignty; modern challenges (globalisation, human rights, supranational bodies).

Lecture 22 — The Nature of International Law ★ Most likely

Rosalyn Higgins (former President of the ICJ): international law governs relations between States, and between States and international organisations — "in a world divided by politics, it is our common language." The big exam question is: is international law really "law"?

Break the name into three elements: Public (traditionally State-to-State, but now reaching individuals and corporations) · International (no longer only "between States"; it permeates domestic law) · Law — but it is consent-based (basado en el consentimiento: los Estados deben aceptarlo), which raises the doubt: is it law in the full sense?

Why it looks different: no centralised institutions

  • Legislative: there is no world parliament → law is shaped by the ILC (Comisión de Derecho Internacional) and UN General Assembly resolutions.
  • Judicial: no unified court system → the ICJ (Corte Internacional de Justicia), the ICC (Corte Penal Internacional), regional courts, arbitration.
  • Executive: no global police → UN peacekeeping forces, regional coalitions (NATO, AU).
Louis Henkin: "Almost all nations observe almost all principles of international law and almost all of their obligations almost all of the time." — the answer to the sceptics: it mostly works.

Basic principles: sovereign equality of States · respect for independence · prohibition on war (UN Charter Art. 2(3)–(4): peaceful settlement is mandatory, force is prohibited; the exception is Art. 51 — the right of individual or collective self-defence (legítima defensa)). Critics from the Global South (Rajagopal) argue it lacks legitimacy, being rooted in Western dominance. What is international law for? An instrument of power ↔ a tool of emancipation ↔ self-preservation of the form ↔ a promise of justice.

🔑 Exam essentials
  • Higgins definition; the three elements (Public/International/Law); consent-based.
  • No parliament/court/police (ILC; ICJ/ICC; peacekeeping); Henkin quote.
  • Sovereign equality · independence · prohibition on war (Art 2(3)-(4) & Art 51).

Lecture 23 — Sources of International Law ★ Most likely

First, a distinction: a formal source (fuente formal) creates binding law (like a statute domestically); a material source (fuente material) does not create law but only evidences its content (custom is the example).

Article 38 of the ICJ Statute — memorise the list

  • International conventions / treaties (tratados)
  • International custom (costumbre internacional) — "evidence of a general practice accepted as law"
  • General principles of law (principios generales del derecho) — e.g. good faith, equity
  • Subsidiary means (medios auxiliares) — judicial decisions and the teachings of qualified publicists (la doctrina de los juristas)

Custom = state practice + opinio juris. State practice (práctica estatal) is what States actually do; opinio juris (opinio juris: la convicción de que esa práctica es jurídicamente obligatoria) is the belief that the practice is required by law. The persistent objector (objetor persistente) — a State that objects consistently from the very start — is not bound by the new rule; but new States are bound by rules that already exist. There is a debate about whether specially affected States count for more (the Nuclear Weapons Advisory Opinion).

Treaties (VCLT Art. 2(1) (Convención de Viena sobre el Derecho de los Tratados)): a written agreement between States, binding only on those who consent. A treaty can relate to custom in four ways — it can bind the parties, develop, crystallise (cristalizar: fijar una costumbre que se estaba formando) or codify custom. Subsidiary sources: no binding precedent in IL (Art. 59 ICJ Statute limits a decision's effect to the parties); soft law (derecho "blando": no vinculante) (GA resolutions, ILC work) is non-binding but helps form custom and fill gaps.

🔑 Exam essentials
  • Formal vs material sources; Article 38's four sources.
  • Custom = state practice + opinio juris; persistent objector.
  • VCLT treaties; the 4 ways a treaty relates to custom (bind/develop/crystallise/codify); soft law.

Lecture 24 — Hierarchy of Norms / Jus Cogens ★ Most likely

Unlike domestic law, international law has no formal hierarchy of sources — this is called relative normativity (normatividad relativa). Value is placed on the content of a rule rather than its source. But two content-based super-categories stand out.

Jus cogens (peremptory norms) (normas imperativas inderogables): norms from which no derogation (de las que no cabe excepción ni pacto en contra) is permitted, whatever a State consents to. VCLT Arts. 53 & 64: any treaty conflicting with jus cogens is void (nulo). They are accepted by the international community of States "as a whole". Examples: slavery & apartheid · crimes against humanity · war crimes · genocide · torture. Case evolution: Nicaragua (1986, "intransgressible principles") → Furundžija (1998) → Congo v Rwanda (2006, genocide) → Belgium v Senegal (2012, torture).

Erga omnes (erga omnes: obligaciones frente a toda la comunidad internacional): obligations owed to the whole community, so any State may invoke responsibility for a breach. All jus cogens norms are erga omnes, but not the reverse — erga omnes is the wider category. When treaties conflict, two principles help: lex specialis (la regla especial prevalece sobre la general) and lex posterior (la regla posterior prevalece sobre la anterior). The proliferation of specialised regimes (trade, human rights, environment) creates fragmentation (fragmentación: distintos tribunales interpretan distinto).

🔑 Exam essentials
  • Relative normativity (no formal hierarchy; content over source).
  • Jus cogens: no derogation; VCLT 53/64 → void treaties; the list; the 4 cases.
  • Erga omnes (all jus cogens are erga omnes, not vice versa); lex specialis / lex posterior; fragmentation.

Lecture 25 — Understanding the European Union ★ Most likely

The EU is a unique creature that pools the sovereignty (pone en común/cede soberanía) of its Member States to gain collective strength. It is neither a federation (like the US) nor a mere international organisation (like the UN) — something genuinely in between.

Legal foundation

  • Treaty-based (basada en Tratados): every EU action must rest on the Treaties.
  • Rule of law: the ECJ (Tribunal de Justicia de la UE) ensures compliance.
  • Direct effect (efecto directo: el Derecho UE crea derechos que los individuos pueden invocar ante los tribunales nacionales).
  • Primacy (primacía: el Derecho UE prevalece sobre el nacional en caso de conflicto).

History: ECSC 1951 (Comunidad del Carbón y del Acero) → Treaties of Rome / EEC 1957 → enlargements (UK/Ireland/Denmark 1973; Spain & Portugal 1986) → Maastricht 1993 (the EU is born) → Amsterdam/Nice → Lisbon 2009 (after the failed Constitutional Treaty). The three-pillar structure (1993–2009): the EC pillar (supranational (supranacional) — internal market, monetary union) plus CFSP (foreign & security policy) and JHA (justice & home affairs), both intergovernmental (intergubernamentales: deciden los Estados, no la UE).

Institutions: European Parliament (directly elected, co-legislates) · Council of the EU (ministers of the Member States) · European Commission (proposes legislation, implements) · Court of Justice (uniform interpretation).

Landmark cases (must know)

  • Van Gend en Loos (1963) → created the doctrine of direct effect (EU law gives individuals rights national courts must protect).
  • Costa v ENEL (1964) → established the primacy of EU law as "an independent source of law".
🔑 Exam essentials
  • Pools sovereignty — neither federation nor ordinary IO; treaty-based / direct effect / primacy.
  • ECSC 1951 · Rome 1957 · Maastricht 1993 · Lisbon 2009; three pillars; institutions.
  • Van Gend en Loos (direct effect) + Costa v ENEL (primacy).

Lecture 26 — The Scope & Intensity of EU Law ★ Most likely

Unlike a sovereign State, the EU can only act on conferred powers (competencias atribuidas) — powers the Member States have explicitly given it. Three principles govern what it may do, when it should, and how far:

  • Enumeration / conferral (principio de atribución): every action needs a legal basis in the Treaties; whatever is not conferred stays with the States.
  • Subsidiarity (subsidiariedad): should the EU act even though it can? Only if the States cannot achieve the objective sufficiently on their own. Enforced by national parliaments' "yellow cards" ("tarjetas amarillas": objeciones de los parlamentos nacionales).
  • Proportionality (proporcionalidad): action must not go beyond what is necessary.

Competences (competencias): exclusive (only the EU — customs union, competition rules, common commercial policy, marine resources) · shared (both can act; the EU prevails in conflict) · complementary/supporting (the EU only supports/coordinates). The ECJ favours teleological interpretation (interpretación teleológica: según la finalidad/el "telos"), which gives it the widest discretion and an expansive view of EU competences.

Sources — Article 288 TFEU (Tratado de Funcionamiento de la UE)

InstrumentEffect
Regulations (reglamentos)General application, binding in their entirety, directly applicable (directamente aplicables, sin transposición) in all Member States.
Directives (directivas)Binding as to the result to be achieved; States choose the form & methods. They require transposition (transposición: incorporarlas al derecho nacional) within a deadline.
Decisions (decisiones)Binding in their entirety (only on the addressees if it names them).
Recommendations / Opinions (recomendaciones / dictámenes)Not binding.
⚠️ Watch out — classic trapRegulation = directly applicable (no transposition). Directive = must be transposed. Do not swap them!
🔑 Exam essentials
  • Conferral + subsidiarity (yellow cards) + proportionality.
  • Exclusive / shared / complementary competences; teleological interpretation.
  • Art. 288: regulations vs directives vs decisions (+ non-binding recommendations/opinions).

Islamic Law — The Shariah

The Shariah (la Sharía: la ley islámica) is the complex of divinely revealed rules (reglas reveladas por Dios) the faithful Muslim must observe. Its sole ground of validity is that it is the manifested will of Allah — it does not depend on any earthly law-giver.

Key contrast with Western law: the West changes its law to fit changing needs; Islam holds the opposite — society must adapt itself to the law, which was revealed once and for all through the Prophet Muhammad. So jurists do not create law; they discover (descubren) a law that already exists. And it covers all of life (prayer, fasting, pilgrimage), not just what interests the State.

The four roots / sources (las cuatro "raíces") — codified by As-Shafi'i (d. 820)

  1. Qur'an — the highest source (yet only a few verses are directly applicable legal rules; most are general ethical precepts).
  2. Sunna (la práctica inspirada del Profeta) — second source; scholars admit it is "largely unhistorical" (Schacht).
  3. Ijma (el consenso de los juristas) — plays the critical role: it alone decides what valid rule can be drawn from the Qur'an or Sunna.
  4. Qiyas (razonamiento por analogía).

Four schools (escuelas jurídicas): Maliki (Medina; N/W/Central Africa) · Hanafi (Kufa, Iraq; Near & Middle East, Indus) · Shafi'i (E. Africa, Malaysia, Indonesia) · Hanbali (Saudi Arabia). Rigidity & hiyal (hiyal: artificios para "esquivar" la ley) — e.g. the "double sale" used to dodge the ban on usury (usura: préstamo con interés) while respecting the letter of the law; the Shariah ended up confined mainly to private law. Modernisation (from 1850): the Majalla (1869–76); most radically, Turkey adopted the Swiss Civil Code in 1926, breaking with Islamic family law; in British India, judges built Anglo-Muhammadan law using common-law principles. The Iranian Revolution (Khomeini) then triggered a reislamification (reislamización del derecho).

🔑 Exam essentials
  • Divine foundation: society adapts to the law; jurists discover, not create.
  • 4 roots (Qur'an / Sunna / Ijma / Qiyas) & 4 schools.
  • hiyal (double sale); Turkey 1926 (Swiss Code); reislamification (Iran).

Law in the Far East

The fundamental difference: Western systems regulate important questions through objective rules of law enforced by courts; in the Far East, informal dispute resolution (resolución informal de conflictos) — conciliation, compromise, arbitration (conciliación, transacción, arbitraje) — matters far more than litigation. This shows that Western assumptions about law are "temporary and local", not universal.

There is no single "Far Eastern legal family": China and Japan are fundamentally different, despite a shared Confucian (confuciano) heritage.

Confucianism & li: Confucius taught that man, Heaven and earth form a harmoniously ordered universe; the rules of proper behaviour — li (li: las normas de conducta confucianas, según el estatus) — are calibrated by status (age, sex, position). Anyone who drags a fellow citizen to court is seen as a disruptive, uncultivated person; virtue lies in forbearance (tolerancia/aguante) and compromise. The Legists vs Confucians (legistas frente a confucianos) struggle: Legists said man is selfish and needs severe penal sanctions; Confucians said man is good and improves through education. The TS'IN dynasty (221 BC) briefly favoured the Legists (burning Confucian texts); the HAN dynasty (206 BC) restored Confucianism as state ideology for ~2000 years.

Chinese codes were almost entirely criminal & administrative; private law (commerce, family, succession) was barely touched and settled outside the courts. Modern China: "unequal treaties" (tratados desiguales) from 1850 → 1911 Republic (the Kuomintang codified using German/Swiss law) → 1949 Mao abolishes it all → the Cultural Revolution (Revolución Cultural) (lawyers persecuted) → Deng Xiaoping's renewal (the Fundamentals of Private Law, 1987). Lubman: without political reform, law in China won't have the value the West gives it. Japan: the Tokugawa Shogunate (1603–1867, feudal/Confucian) → the Meiji opening (1853 US warships; 1889 Prussian-style constitution; the 1898 Civil Code based on the German BGB) → strong American influence after WWII (1946 constitution). Yet "living law" (el "derecho vivo": cómo funciona en la práctica) kept favouring conciliation ("invisible accounts" of mutual obligation); Haley/Oda argue low litigation reflects overburdened courts, not only Confucian culture. Lesson: reception ≠ implementation (adoptar un código ≠ aplicarlo de verdad).

🔑 Exam essentials
  • Conciliation > litigation; no single family (China ≠ Japan).
  • Confucianism & li; Legists vs Confucians (TS'IN / HAN).
  • China (1949 / Cultural Revolution / Deng 1987); Japan (BGB 1898 + US post-1945); "reception ≠ implementation".
