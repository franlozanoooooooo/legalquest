Lecture 1 — What is Law?

Law is a multidimensional phenomenon — there is no single definition. Hart (The Concept of Law): the question "What is law?" has been answered in "diverse, strange and even paradoxical ways" like no other discipline. Bobbio: every definition contains "an element of arbitrariness".

The three dimensions

DimensionMeaning
Law as a human phenomenon (fact)Arises from concrete social realities — context, ideology, conflict. Historical character: it evolves with society.
Law as societal orderingSets patterns/models of behaviour (Nudge theory; smoking bans). Cicero: "We are slaves of the law so that we may be free."
Law as a system of valuesEmbodies views on justice and moral rightness. No legal system is politically neutral.

The two great functions — Lord Denning

"The law has two great objects: to preserve order and to do justice; and the two do not always coincide."
  • Order: necessity of law; legal certainty = certainty about (1) the content of the law, (2) that it will be enforced, (3) that it will be applied consistently.
  • Justice: balance/proportion; equal treatment (treat like cases alike); differentiation (treat different cases differently).

Key case — Bellinger v Bellinger (2001/2003)

A transsexual woman sought recognition of her marriage. The majority held the biological factors from Corbett v Corbett (chromosomal, gonadal, genital) were determinative, and that changing the definition of "female" was a matter for Parliament, not the courts (public policy). The dissent felt "the world had moved on". → tension between order/legal certainty and justice.

🔑 Exam essentials
  • 3 dimensions: fact / system of norms / system of values.
  • Denning: order vs justice (don't always coincide).
  • Legal certainty = content + enforcement + consistency.
  • Bellinger/Corbett: definition of "female" is for Parliament.
⚠️ Watch out"No legal system is politically neutral" is TRUE. Don't confuse order with justice.

Lecture 2 — A Multiplicity of Legal Systems

Law is a product of society: each system rests on its own traditions and culture and is constantly evolving.

Western legal tradition — 4 features

  • Clear demarcation between legal institutions and others.
  • Rule of law — legal authority is supreme over political institutions.
  • Legal doctrine as the principal source and basis of practice.
  • Coherent body with internal logic.
Rule of law: "no one is above the law" — a rational system of general, clear, prospective and impartial rules; an indispensable curb on abuse of power, securing fairness, transparency and equal access to justice.

The legal families

FamilyNotes
Civil law (codified)French (France, Belgium, Spain, Italy…), German (Germany, Austria, Switzerland, Japan, S. Korea, Taiwan), Scandinavian, Chinese (civil + socialist).
Common lawUnwritten / non-textual, case-based, governed by the doctrine of precedent.
Mixed / hybride.g. South Africa = Roman-Dutch law + English common law.

Divisions of law

  • Public vs Private: public = the State plays a role (constitutional, administrative, criminal); private = it does not (contract, property, tort). EU law blurs the line.
  • Substantive vs Procedural: substantive gives rights; procedural gives the rules of court procedure and judicial organisation.
🔑 Exam essentials
  • 4 features of the Western tradition; rule of law is the key one.
  • Civil vs common vs mixed; public/private; substantive/procedural.
⚠️ Watch outJapan is German civil law, not French. Common law is "unwritten" even though today it is heavily statutory.

Lecture 3 — Exploring Normativity

Law is a normative system: it dictates conduct as compulsory, prohibited or allowed. It belongs to the realm of "what ought to be".

Law builds behavioural models through language:

  • Descriptive statements → describe reality ("what is"); can be true or false.
  • Prescriptive statements → prescribe conduct ("what ought to be"); cannot be true or false.

Typical exercise: classify articles. e.g. "The capital of Spain is Madrid" → descriptive; "Non-emancipated minors cannot marry" → prescriptive.

🔑 Exam essentials
  • Compulsory / prohibited / allowed.
  • Descriptive (true/false) vs prescriptive (not true/false).

Lecture 4 — Normative Dimension II: Validity / Efficacy / Justice

The three criteria used to analyse a legal rule — the pillar of the course.

CriterionWhat it assesses
ValidityRelational concept: a norm is valid because it belongs to a legal system. It is binding and backed by the State's enforcement machinery. Needs formal and material requirements.
EfficacyIs it obeyed in practice? Looks at obedience and enforcement (punishment for breach).
JusticeAdaptation to an ideal system of values (the axiological dimension); traditionally the ultimate goal of law.

Reasons for obeying the law: habit · avoiding punishment · agreement · self-interest.

Compliance spectrum: spontaneous → threat-induced → non-compliance despite threat → complete non-compliance.

⚠️ Watch outEfficacy (is it obeyed/punished?) ≠ Efficiency (did it achieve its goal?).
🔑 Exam essentials
  • Define validity (relational, formal + material), efficacy, justice.
  • Efficacy ≠ efficiency.

Lecture 5 — Normative Dimension III: Conceptual Independence

  • At the norm level → conceptual independence: validity, efficacy and justice are independent → all 8 combinations exist (valid–ineffective–unjust, valid–effective–just, etc.).
  • At the legal-system level → conceptual interdependence:
    • Validity & effectiveness: a system must be broadly effective to exist.
    • Validity & justice: democratic systems positivise justice in their superior norms (e.g. Art. 1.1 of the Constitution, Title I). A norm, to be valid, cannot contradict them.
🔑 Exam essentials
  • Independence at norm level (8 combos) vs interdependence at system level.

Lecture 8 — Legal Realism

Law = the standards enforced by the courts. Realism reduces validity to efficacy: valid norms are those judges actually apply.

American RealismScandinavian Realism
Pragmatist & behaviourist; "law in action"; focused on the judicial process.Philosophical attack on the metaphysical foundations of law.

Both reject the conflation of law and morality and distrust absolutes like "justice".

Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr: "The prophecies of what the courts will do in fact… are what I mean by the law." — law as prophecy. And: "The life of the law has not been logic: it has been experience."
  • Decisions often rest on hunches/biases; rules are indeterminate; bigger role for the social sciences.
  • Reaction against legal formalism / mechanical jurisprudence. Case: US v E.C. Knight Co (Sugar Trust) — decided "on labels", ignoring that 98% control of sugar refining affects interstate commerce.
  • Hungry judge effect: physiological factors (hunger/fatigue) influence rulings.
  • Scandinavian (Alf Ross): law = directives addressed to judges; legal science is empirical; "N is the law in force in country P" predicts judges will apply N. Influence of logical empiricism / falsifiability (McLean v Arkansas: "creation science" is not falsifiable → not science).
🔑 Exam essentials
  • Validity → efficacy; law = what courts do (prediction).
  • Holmes (law as prophecy; experience over logic).
  • American vs Scandinavian (Alf Ross); Sugar Trust; hungry judge; McLean v Arkansas.

Lecture 9 — Justice & Legal Systems

Justice is a moral concept — a subset of morality requiring consciousness, rationality and a moral sense.

Alf Ross (sceptic): "To invoke justice is the same thing as banging on the table: an emotional expression which turns one's demand into an absolute postulate."

Just/unjust can be predicated of agents (a just man), actions (a just decision) and states of affairs (a just legal system). Suffering from natural disasters is not in itself injustice.

The three types of justice

TypeWhat it is
CorrectiveRectifying harm between two parties — restoring equilibrium (tort/contract; the scales and blindfold of the statue of Justice).
DistributiveFair allocation of goods — "giving each their due". Values: freedom, equality, solidarity. The legislator's concern.
FormalLegal certainty: consistent application of rules; depersonalisation → rules apply to the act, not the actor.

Manifestations of formal justice: against power (habeas corpus, in dubio pro reo); in the system (validity criteria — back to Fuller); in society (private property, binding contracts).

🔑 Exam essentials
  • Ross "banging on the table"; agents/actions/states of affairs.
  • Corrective / distributive (freedom-equality-solidarity) / formal (act not actor).

Lecture 10 — Theories of Justice

1) Utilitarianism

Bentham: pain & pleasure as "sovereign masters"; the principle of utility = maximise happiness/preferences. Act utilitarianism (judge each action by its consequences) vs Rule utilitarianism (judge the rule everyone should follow). Transplant dilemma: sacrifice 1 healthy person to save 5? Act = yes; Rule = no (preserves trust in medicine). Critiques: devaluation of personhood, distribution, majoritarianism.

2) Rawls — Justice as Fairness

An alternative to utilitarianism. Veil of ignorance: design society without knowing your position → impartial = legitimate. Maximin: choose the scheme that maximises the minimum position. Two principles: (1) equal basic liberties for all; (2) democratic equality of opportunity (reduce the arbitrary advantages of the "natural lottery").

3) Nozick — Libertarianism

Minimal state; distributive justice is irrelevant. Entitlement theory: a distribution is just if reached through voluntary exchange or gift. Liberty vs equality are incompatible — maintaining equality requires violating liberty.

🔑 Exam essentials
  • Act vs rule utilitarianism + transplant dilemma + 3 critiques.
  • Rawls: veil of ignorance, maximin, two principles.
  • Nozick: minimal state, entitlement theory, liberty vs equality.

Lecture 11 — The Civil Law Tradition: Roman Law

The civil law tradition is the world's oldest and most widespread. Three pillars: Roman law, Germanic customary law, Canon law.

Periods: Archaic → Pre-classical → Classical (Principate) → Post-classical. Twelve Tables (450 BC) = first codification, public, lasting ~1000 years. Lex Hortensia (287 BC); ius honorarium (created by the praetors, flexible); Praetor Peregrinus (242 BC) → ius gentium. Constitutio Antoniniana (AD 212): citizenship to all free inhabitants.

Body of lawMeaning
Ius civileLaw of Roman citizens (principle of personality of law).
Ius gentiumInstitutions common to all peoples under Rome.
Ius honorariumLaw created by magistrates when applying the law — progressive, flexible.

Corpus Juris Civilis (Justinian, 534): Codex (imperial legislation) · Digest (the largest part — excerpts of jurists) · Institutions (student textbook).

Ius privatum = law of persons / things / actions.

🔑 Exam essentials
  • 3 pillars; Twelve Tables 450 BC; ius civile/gentium/honorarium.
  • Corpus Juris Civilis = Codex/Digest/Institutions.

Lecture 12 — Ius Commune & the Codification of Law

  • 476 AD: fall of the Western Roman Empire → legal fragmentation; Roman law survives in vulgarised form, mixed with Germanic custom.
  • Revival (11th–12th c.): rediscovery of Justinian's Digest; struggle between Empire and Papacy; rise of the universities (Bologna, Paris, Salamanca) producing professional jurists.
  • Ius commune = Roman + Canon + Germanic custom — a universalising factor across Continental Europe.
  • Humanists + the School of Natural Law (Grotius, Pufendorf, Thomasius, Wolff) provide the ideological basis for codification.
CodeFeatures
French Civil Code 1804 (Code Napoléon)Unifies French law; three pillars = private property, freedom of contract, the patriarchal family; broad, flexible rules leaving room for the judge.
German Civil Code / BGB 19005 books (general, obligations, property, family, succession); conceptualism (precise definitions); driven by national unification. More modern than the French Code.
🔑 Exam essentials
  • 476 → revival (Digest/Bologna) → ius commune = Roman+Canon+Germanic.
  • French Code 1804 (3 pillars) vs German BGB 1900 (5 books, conceptualism).

Lecture 14 — The Common Law Tradition: History

Bentham: the common law is "a thing merely imaginary." Austin: "childish fiction… made by nobody" (attacking the declaratory theory that judges only "declare" pre-existing law).
  • Pre-Norman England: Germanic folk-law. The Norman Conquest (1066) began centralisation.
  • Henry II (1154–89): divided England into circuits and sent royal judges → law "common" to the whole country.
  • Writ system: an action began with a writ from the chancery; each writ became a precedent; from the 14th c. writs issued only if facts matched earlier cases (Register of Writs); no suitable writ → no remedy. The forms of action classified and ossified claims → rigidity → injustice → the rise of equity.
  • 16th-century crisis (pressure to receive Roman law) survived thanks to 4 factors: Tudor monarchs reshaped institutions; new courts (Chancery, Star Chamber) covered gaps; Coke's restatement modernised principles; the Inns of Court kept "the toughness of a taught tradition" (Maitland).
🔑 Exam essentials
  • Bentham/Austin (declaratory theory); Henry II + circuits.
  • Writ system / forms of action / Register of Writs → rigidity → equity.
  • 16th-c. crisis + 4 survival factors (Tudor, new courts, Coke, Inns of Court).

Lecture 15 — The Rise of Equity

After the Norman Conquest the common law grew out of the curia regis. Shortcomings that created the need for equity:

  • Limited writs — no suitable writ, no remedy.
  • Inflexibility — rigidity producing unconscionable results.
  • Corruption & intimidation — courts influenced by the powerful.

Equity = fairness/justice. The wronged party would petition the monarch ("the fountain of justice") appealing to conscience → referred to the Lord Chancellor → the Court of Chancery (late 14th c.). Systematisation: Lord Nottingham (1675), Lord Hardwicke (1737), Lord Eldon (1801, precedent).

Conflict resolved under James I (Earl of Oxford's Case, 1615): equity prevails. Judicature Acts 1873–75: one Supreme Court; any division applies common law and equity; in conflict, equity prevails. Merger debate — Ashburner: "the two streams… run side by side and do not mingle their waters" (not fused). Maxims (e.g. "he who comes to equity must come with clean hands"). Equitable remedies: injunction, specific performance, rescission, rectification, account.

🔑 Exam essentials
  • 3 shortcomings → Chancellor/Chancery/conscience.
  • Judicature Acts 1873–75 (equity prevails); merger debate (Ashburner); maxims; clean hands.

Lecture 19 — Sovereignty: From Westphalia to Globalization ★ Most likely

Peace of Westphalia (1648) ended several wars and divided Europe into sovereign Nation-States with exclusive power over their territory → law became national law. Westphalian duo: the only two forms of law = national State law + international public law.

Elements of the State: sovereignty · government · territory & population.

Classical theories of sovereignty

AuthorThesis
Jean Bodin (16th c.)Sovereignty = "absolute and perpetual power"; strong monarchy.
Thomas Hobbes (1651)Absolute sovereignty via the social contract — liberty traded for security.
John Locke (1689)Limited sovereignty; government rests on the consent of the people.
J.-J. Rousseau (1762)Popular sovereignty; the general will guides government.

Types: internal/external · legal/political · negative/positive. Challenges: globalization & economic integration; international human rights; supranational institutions (EU, WTO, UN). Shift from natural law to legal positivism. Constitutional milestones: Magna Carta 1215 → Bill of Rights 1689 → US Constitution 1787 → French Revolution 1789.

🔑 Exam essentials
  • Westphalia 1648 + Westphalian duo.
  • Bodin / Hobbes / Locke / Rousseau (who says what).
  • Types of sovereignty + modern challenges.

Lecture 22 — The Nature of International Law ★ Most likely

Rosalyn Higgins: the law governing relations between States and between States and international organisations — "in a world divided by politics, it is our common language."

Three elements: Public · International · Law — but consent-based (is it "law" in the full sense?).

No centralised institutions

  • Legislative: no world parliament → ILC, UN General Assembly resolutions.
  • Judicial: no unified court → ICJ, ICC, regional courts, arbitration.
  • Executive: no global police → UN peacekeeping, regional coalitions (NATO, AU).
Louis Henkin: "Almost all nations observe almost all principles of international law and almost all of their obligations almost all of the time."

Basic principles: sovereign equality of States · respect for independence · prohibition on war (UN Charter Art. 2(3)-(4); exception Art. 51 self-defence). Critiques from the Global South (Rajagopal: rooted in Western dominance). What is IL for? instrument of power ↔ tool of emancipation ↔ self-preservation of form ↔ promise of justice.

🔑 Exam essentials
  • Higgins definition; Public/International/Law; consent-based.
  • No parliament/court/police (ILC, ICJ/ICC, peacekeeping); Henkin quote.
  • Sovereign equality · independence · prohibition on war (Art 2 & 51).

Lecture 23 — Sources of International Law ★ Most likely

Formal sources create binding law; material sources only evidence its content (e.g. custom).

Article 38 ICJ Statute (memorise)

  • International conventions / treaties
  • International custom — "evidence of a general practice accepted as law"
  • General principles of law (good faith, equity)
  • Subsidiary means — judicial decisions & the teachings of publicists (doctrine)

Custom = state practice + opinio juris. Persistent objector: a State objecting consistently from the start is not bound. New States are bound by existing rules. Debate on specially affected States (Nuclear Weapons Advisory Opinion).

Treaties (VCLT Art. 2(1)): a written agreement between States; bind only those who consent. A treaty can bind parties, develop, crystallise or codify custom. Subsidiary: no binding precedent (Art. 59 ICJ); soft law (GA resolutions, ILC) — non-binding but shapes custom.

🔑 Exam essentials
  • Article 38 (4 sources); custom = practice + opinio juris.
  • Persistent objector; the 4 ways a treaty relates to custom.

Lecture 24 — Hierarchy of Norms / Jus Cogens ★ Most likely

Relative normativity: international law has no formal hierarchy of sources — value lies in the content of rules.

Jus cogens (peremptory norms): norms from which no derogation is permitted, regardless of consent. VCLT Arts. 53 & 64: a treaty conflicting with jus cogens is void. Accepted by the international community "as a whole".

Examples: slavery & apartheid · crimes against humanity · war crimes · genocide · torture. Case evolution: Nicaragua (1986) → Furundžija (1998) → Congo v Rwanda (2006, genocide) → Belgium v Senegal (2012, torture).

Erga omnes: obligations owed to the whole international community — any State may invoke responsibility. All jus cogens are erga omnes, but not vice versa. Treaty conflicts: lex specialis, lex posterior. Fragmentation of specialised regimes.

🔑 Exam essentials
  • Jus cogens (VCLT 53/64; list; void treaties; the 4 cases).
  • Erga omnes; lex specialis / lex posterior; fragmentation.

Lecture 25 — Understanding the European Union ★ Most likely

The EU pools the sovereignty of its Member States — neither a federation (like the US) nor a mere international organisation (like the UN).

Legal foundation

  • Treaty-based · Rule of law (the ECJ ensures compliance) · Direct effect (creates rights for individuals) · Primacy over national law.

History: ECSC 1951 → Treaties of Rome / EEC 1957 → enlargements (UK/Ireland/Denmark 1973; Spain & Portugal 1986) → Maastricht 1993 (EU born) → Amsterdam/Nice → Lisbon 2009. Three pillars (1993–2009): EC (supranational) · CFSP & JHA (intergovernmental).

Institutions: European Parliament (elected, co-legislates) · Council of the EU (ministers) · European Commission (proposes & executes) · Court of Justice (uniform interpretation).

Landmark cases

  • Van Gend en Loos (1963) → doctrine of direct effect.
  • Costa v ENEL (1964) → primacy of EU law ("an independent source of law").
🔑 Exam essentials
  • Pools sovereignty (not a federation, not an IO); treaty-based/direct effect/primacy.
  • ECSC 1951 / Rome 1957 / Maastricht 1993 / Lisbon 2009; 3 pillars; institutions.
  • Van Gend en Loos (direct effect) + Costa v ENEL (primacy).

Lecture 26 — The Scope & Intensity of EU Law ★ Most likely

The EU acts only on conferred powers. Three governing principles:

  • Enumeration / conferral: every action needs a legal basis in the Treaties; whatever is not conferred stays with the States.
  • Subsidiarity: should the EU act even though it can? Only if the States cannot achieve the objective sufficiently. Enforcement: national parliaments' "yellow cards".
  • Proportionality: action must not exceed what is necessary.

Competences: exclusive (only the EU — customs union, competition, common commercial policy, marine resources) · shared (EU prevails in conflict) · complementary. Teleological interpretation: the ECJ reads by purpose (telos) → expansive view of competences.

Sources — Article 288 TFEU

InstrumentEffect
RegulationsGeneral application, binding in entirety, directly applicable (no transposition).
DirectivesBinding as to the result; States choose form & methods (transposition with a deadline).
DecisionsBinding in entirety (only on addressees if specified).
Recommendations / OpinionsNot binding.
⚠️ Watch outRegulation = directly applicable; Directive = must be transposed. Don't swap them!
🔑 Exam essentials
  • Conferral + subsidiarity (yellow cards) + proportionality.
  • Exclusive / shared / complementary; teleological interpretation.
  • Art. 288: regulations vs directives vs decisions.

Islamic Law — The Shariah

The Shariah is the complex of divinely revealed rules the faithful must observe; its sole ground of validity is that it is the manifested will of Allah — it does not depend on any earthly law-giver.

Key difference from Western law: the West changes law to fit needs; Islam holds that society must adapt itself to the law (revealed once and for all through Muhammad). Jurists do not create law — they discover it. It covers all of life.

The four roots / sources (As-Shafi'i, d. 820)

  1. Qur'an — highest source (but few verses are directly applicable legal rules).
  2. Sunna — inspired practice of the Prophet (largely "unhistorical" — Schacht).
  3. Ijma — consensus of scholars; the critical role.
  4. Qiyas — reasoning by analogy.

Four schools: Maliki (Medina; N/W/Central Africa) · Hanafi (Kufa, Iraq; Near/Middle East, Indus) · Shafi'i (E. Africa, Malaysia, Indonesia) · Hanbali (Saudi Arabia).

Rigidity & hiyal (legal evasion, e.g. the "double sale" to dodge the ban on usury). Modernisation (Majalla 1869–76; Turkey → Swiss Civil Code 1926; Anglo-Muhammadan law in British India). Reislamification after the Iranian Revolution (Khomeini).

🔑 Exam essentials
  • Divine foundation (society adapts to the law); jurists discover, not create.
  • 4 roots (Qur'an/Sunna/Ijma/Qiyas) & 4 schools; hiyal; Turkey 1926; reislamification.

Law in the Far East

Fundamental difference: the West regulates important questions through objective rules of law enforced by courts; in the Far East informal dispute resolution (conciliation, compromise, arbitration) is far more important than litigation.

There is no single "Far Eastern legal family": China and Japan are fundamentally different, despite a shared Confucian heritage.

Confucianism & li: a harmoniously ordered universe; rules of proper behaviour (li) calibrated by status. Whoever drags a fellow citizen to court is seen as disruptive and uncultivated; virtue lies in forbearance. Legists vs Confucians: the TS'IN dynasty (221 BC) favoured the Legists (burning Confucian texts); the HAN dynasty (206 BC) restored Confucianism as state ideology for ~2000 years.

Chinese codes: mainly criminal & administrative; private law almost absent (resolved outside the courts). Modern China: unequal treaties (1850) → 1911 Republic (Kuomintang: German/Swiss law) → 1949 Mao → Cultural Revolution (lawyers persecuted) → Deng (Fundamentals of Private Law, 1987). Lubman: without political reform, law won't have the value the West gives it.

Japan: Tokugawa (1603–1867, feudal/Confucian) → Meiji (1853 US ships; 1889 Prussian-style constitution; 1898 Civil Code based on the German BGB) → post-WWII American influence (1946 constitution). Living law: conciliation persists ("invisible accounts"); Haley/Oda argue low litigation reflects overburdened courts, not just Confucianism. Reception ≠ implementation.

🔑 Exam essentials
  • Conciliation > litigation; no single family (China ≠ Japan).
  • Confucianism/li; Legists vs Confucians (TS'IN/HAN).
  • China (1949 / Cultural Revolution / Deng 1987); Japan (BGB 1898 + US post-1945); "reception ≠ implementation".
