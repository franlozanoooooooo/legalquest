1 What is Law?

Core idea: Law is a multidimensional phenomenon — at once a fact, a system of norms and a system of values.

  • Hart (The Concept of Law): no question has been answered in so many diverse ways as "What is law?" → law is essentially contested.
  • Bobbio: every definition of law contains an "element of arbitrariness" when fixing its scope.

The three dimensions

DimensionMeaning
Law as a human phenomenon (fact)Arises from concrete social realities (context, ideology, conflicts). Historical character → it evolves.
Law as societal orderingSets patterns of behaviour (cf. Nudge Theory). Cicero: "We are slaves of the law so that we may be free."
Law as a system of valuesEmbodies justice and moral rightness. No legal system is politically neutral.

The functions of law — Lord Denning

"The law has two great objects: to preserve order and to do justice; and the two do not always coincide."
  • Order: necessity of law; legal certainty = certainty about (1) the content of the law, (2) that it will be enforced, (3) that it will be applied consistently.
  • Justice: balance/proportion; treat like cases alike; treat different cases differently.

Key case — Bellinger v Bellinger (2001/2003): a transsexual woman seeks recognition of her marriage. The majority followed Corbett v Corbett (chromosomal/gonadal/genital factors are decisive) and held that changing the definition of "female" is a matter for Parliament, not the courts (public policy). The dissent argued "the world had moved on." → illustrates the order/certainty vs justice tension.

🔑 Exam essentials3 dimensions · order vs justice (Denning) · legal certainty (3 certainties) · Bellinger / Corbett.
⚠️ Common traps"Politically neutral" is FALSE. Don't confuse order with justice.

2 A Multiplicity of Legal Systems (Families)

Core idea: law is a product of society; each system has its own traditions and keeps evolving.

Western Legal Tradition — 4 features

  1. Clear demarcation between legal institutions and others.
  2. Rule of Law — legal authority is supreme over political institutions.
  3. Legal doctrine as the principal source.
  4. Coherent body with internal logic.
Rule of Law: "no one is above the law." A system of general, clear, prospective and impartial rules; an indispensable curb on the abuse of power, securing fairness, transparency, equal access to justice.

The families

  • Civil Law (codified): French (France, Belgium, Spain, Italy…), German (Germany, Austria, Switzerland, Japan, Korea, Taiwan), Scandinavian (Sweden, Denmark, Norway, Iceland), Chinese (civil + socialist).
  • Common Law: unwritten / non-textual, built on cases, governed by the doctrine of precedent.
  • Mixed / Hybrid: e.g. South Africa = Roman-Dutch + English Common Law.

Divisions of law

DivisionCategories
Public vs PrivatePublic (the State is involved): constitutional, administrative, criminal. Private: contract, property, tort. EU Law blurs the line.
Substantive vs ProceduralSubstantive: grants rights. Procedural: rules of court procedure and the organisation of the judiciary.
🔑 Exam essentials4 features of the Western tradition · rule of law · civil vs common vs mixed · public/private · substantive/procedural.
⚠️ Common trapsJapan is German civil law, not French. Common law is "unwritten" even though today it is heavily statutory.

3 The Normative Dimension — Validity / Efficacy / Justice

Normativity & language

  • Law commands behaviour as compulsory / prohibited / allowed. It belongs to the realm of the "ought to be."
  • Descriptive statements → "what is", can be true/false. Prescriptive statements → "what ought to be", cannot be true/false.

Validity / Efficacy / Justice — the pillar of the course

CriterionWhat it assesses
ValidityA relational concept: a norm is valid because it belongs to a legal system; it is binding and backed by the State. Requires formal and material requirements.
EfficacyIs it complied with? Looks at obedience and enforcement.
JusticeAdequacy to an ideal system of values (the axiological dimension); traditionally the ultimate goal of law.

Reasons for obeying: habit · avoiding punishment · agreement · self-interest.
Compliance spectrum: spontaneous → threat-induced → non-compliance despite threat → complete non-compliance.

⚠️ Common trapsEfficacy (is it obeyed / punished?) ≠ Efficiency (did it achieve its goal?).

Conceptual independence

  • At the NORM level → independence: the 8 combinations of valid / effective / just.
  • At the SYSTEM level → interdependence: democratic systems positivise justice in their superior norms (e.g. Art. 1.1 of a constitution); a norm, to be valid, cannot contradict them.
🔑 Exam essentialsDefine validity/efficacy/justice · prescriptive vs descriptive · efficacy ≠ efficiency · independence (norm) vs interdependence (system).

4 Natural Law Already examined — low priority

Three schools of legal theory: Natural Law (law–morality connection), Legal Positivism (law = human construct, validity from formal processes), Legal Realism (what judges actually do).

Natural law starts from justice and a higher law (divine revelation, human nature, reason).

Two assumptions: (1) a cognitivist position in meta-ethics (true, universal moral principles exist, knowable by reason); (2) a dualist position (objective Natural Law ↔ human-made Positive Law).

How the principles are known:

  • Naturalism (nature + reason): Cicero ("true law is right reason in agreement with nature… one eternal law for all nations and times"); Aquinas.
  • Intuitionism (moral intuition): Grotius (valid "even if there were no God," like 2+2=4); Finnis — basic goods: life, knowledge, play, aesthetic experience, sociability/friendship, practical reasonableness, religion.

Non-cognitivism (the opposite): emotivism, moral relativism, epistemic limitation.

Natural Law ↔ Positive Law

  • Aquinas: Lex iniusta non est lex (an unjust law is not law) — but disobedience is justified only to avoid scandal/disorder.
  • Finnis: unjust laws are not "law" in the fullest sense.
  • Lon Fuller — internal morality of law: the tale of King Rex and his 8 failures: (1) no consistent rules; (2) no promulgation; (3) retroactivity; (4) incomprehensible rules; (5) contradictions; (6) demanding the impossible; (7) constant change; (8) mismatch between rule and administration. → His 8 principles: generality, promulgation, non-retroactivity, clarity, non-contradiction, possibility of compliance, constancy, congruence. The test is procedural, not one of moral content.

Case — R v Dudley and Stephens (1884): shipwreck of the Mignonette; necessity is NOT a valid defence to murder (Lord Coleridge).

🔑 Exam essentialscognitivism/dualism · naturalism vs intuitionism · Lex iniusta non est lex · Fuller (King Rex, 8 principles, procedural) · Dudley & Stephens · Finnis' basic goods.
⚠️ Common trapsFuller = internal/procedural morality, not moral content. Grotius = intuitionism.

5 Legal Positivism Already examined — low priority

A modern conception (18th–19th c., dominant in the 20th). Against natural law (which centres on justice), positivism centres on validity.

John Austin: "The existence of law is one thing; its merit or demerit is another."

Reaction against natural law

  • Non-cognitivism in ethics: if morality has no rational solution, natural-law principles are mere subjective opinion.
  • Hume's critique (is/ought gap): you cannot logically derive an "ought" from an "is." The natural-law syllogism ("all animals breed / humankind is an animal → humankind ought to breed") smuggles a new term (ought) into the conclusion = the naturalistic fallacy.

Core theses

  • Social thesis: law is a social fact / convention.
  • Separability thesis: no necessary connection between law and morality.
  • Monist position: "law" applies only to positive law; call the rest "morality"/"justice", not "law".
  • Kelsen: "I cannot say what justice is… I must accept a relative justice."
  • Descriptive & "neutral": a theory about the nature of law, no prescriptions; rooted in empiricism.

The problem of evil regimes (post-WWII)

  • Radbruch: it was legal positivism ("law is law, however unjust") that helped German jurists fail to resist Nazi law.
  • Garzón Valdés: the opposite — most pro-Nazi jurists acted from a distorted natural law.
  • Emancipatory power: positivists reply they fight injustice better by exposing law as contingent and changeable ("could always have been otherwise" → if broken, it ought to be fixed). Bentham: law is something men add to the world, not find within it.

Inclusive vs Exclusive Legal Positivism

Inclusive LP (ILP)Exclusive LP (ELP)
Morality as a validity criterionCAN be, if the system incorporates itNEVER: morality cannot be a validity criterion
Validity based onsocial facts + (sometimes) incorporated moralityonly social facts / pedigree (competent authority, procedure)
🔑 Exam essentialsAustin (existence vs merit) · Hume is/ought · social + separability thesis · monism · Kelsen · evil regimes (Radbruch vs Garzón Valdés) · ILP vs ELP.
⚠️ Common trapsSeparability = no necessary connection (not that there cannot be one → that is ILP). Positivism is descriptive.

6 Legal Realism

Thesis: law = the standards courts actually apply. Realism reduces validity to efficacy: valid norms are those judges apply.

American RealismScandinavian Realism
Pragmatic & behaviourist; "law in action"; focused on the judicial process.A philosophical attack on the metaphysical foundations of law.

Both reject the law–morality conflation and distrust absolute values like "justice".

  • Oliver Wendell Holmes Jr: law as prophecy → "The prophecies of what the courts will do in fact… are what I mean by the law." And: "The life of the law has not been logic: it has been experience."
  • Features: decisions driven by hunches/biases; rules are indeterminate and not neutral; bigger role for the social sciences.
  • Reaction against legal formalism / mechanical jurisprudence. Case Sugar Trust (US v E.C. Knight Co): decided "on labels", ignoring that controlling 98% of sugar refining affected interstate commerce.
  • Hungry judge effect: physiological factors (hunger/fatigue) sway decisions → shows the weight of the extra-legal.
  • Scandinavian (Alf Ross): law = directives addressed to judges; legal science is empirical/factual ("N is the law in force in country P" = a prediction that judges will apply N). Influence of logical empiricism / falsifiability (McLean v Arkansas: "creation science" is not science because not falsifiable).
🔑 Exam essentialsvalidity→efficacy · Holmes (law as prophecy; logic vs experience) · American vs Scandinavian (Alf Ross) · Sugar Trust · hungry judge · McLean v Arkansas.
⚠️ Common trapsRealism = law is what judges do (a prediction), not what the books say.

7 Justice & Legal Systems

The third dimension (law as values). Justice is a subset of morality.

Alf Ross (sceptic): "To invoke justice is the same thing as banging on the table: an emotional expression which turns one's demand into an absolute postulate." → the "ideology of justice" breeds intransigence.
  • Justice requires consciousness, rationality and a moral sense. Just/unjust is predicated of: agents (a just man), actions (a just decision), states of affairs (a just system).
  • Suffering from natural disasters is not injustice in itself; injustice arises from failing to relieve it or helping some at others' expense.

Three types of justice

TypeWhat it is
Corrective justiceRectifying harm between two parties (restore the balance; tort/contract; the scales of the statue of Justice; the blindfold = impartiality).
Distributive justiceFair allocation of goods — "giving each person his or her due." Values: freedom, equality, solidarity. Concerns the legislator.
Formal justiceLegal certainty: consistent application of rules; depersonalisation → rules apply to the act, not the actor.

Manifestations of formal justice: against power (habeas corpus, in dubio pro reo); in the system (validity criteria = legal certainty, back to Fuller); in society (private property, binding force of contracts). Interaction corrective ↔ distributive (cases: insurance fraud, squatting amid a housing crisis).

🔑 Exam essentialsRoss "banging on the table" · agents/actions/states of affairs · corrective / distributive (freedom-equality-solidarity) / formal (act not actor) · habeas corpus, in dubio pro reo.

8 Theories of Justice (Rawls / Nozick)

Three theories answer why justice matters and how the system reflects it.

1) Utilitarianism

  • Bentham: pain & pleasure as "sovereign masters"; the principle of utility = maximise happiness/preferences.
  • Act utilitarianism (judge each action by its consequences) vs Rule utilitarianism (judge the rule everyone should follow — more relevant in law).
  • Transplant dilemma: sacrifice 1 healthy person to save 5? Act: yes (5>1). Rule: no ("doctors should never kill a healthy patient" → preserves trust in medicine).
  • Criticisms: devaluation of personhood; distribution problem; majoritarianism (sacrifices minorities).

2) Rawls — Justice as Fairness

  • An alternative to utilitarianism (which fails to protect basic rights).
  • Veil of ignorance: design society without knowing your position (gender, wealth, race, talents…) → impartial principles = legitimate.
  • Maximin: choose the scheme that maximises the minimum position (best outcome for the worst-off).
  • Two principles: (1) equal basic liberties for all; (2) democratic equality of opportunity (reduce the arbitrary advantages of the "natural lottery"; e.g. education).

3) Nozick — Libertarianism

  • Minimal state; distributive justice is irrelevant.
  • Entitlement theory: the current distribution is just if reached through voluntary exchange or gift ("no central distribution").
  • Liberty vs Equality: incompatible — maintaining equality requires intervening and violating liberty.
🔑 Exam essentialsact vs rule utilitarianism + transplant dilemma + 3 criticisms · Rawls (veil of ignorance, maximin, 2 principles) · Nozick (minimal state, entitlement, liberty vs equality).
⚠️ Common trapsRawls = alternative to utilitarianism. Nozick = the opposite of Rawls (rejects patterns of distribution).

9 Civil Law Tradition I — Roman Law

The civil law tradition is the oldest and most widespread (Continental Europe, Latin America, Africa, Asia).

Three pillars

  1. Roman Law (via Justinian's 6th-c. codification).
  2. Germanic customary law.
  3. Canon Law (Church law, partly derived from Roman law).

Periods & milestones

Archaic → Pre-Classical → Classical (Principate) → Post-Classical (to Justinian).

  • Mores maiorum (custom); paterfamilias; Twelve Tables (450 BC) = first codification of Roman civil law, public, in force ~1000 years.
  • Lex Hortensia (287 BC); ius honorarium (created by the praetors, flexible); Praetor Peregrinus (242 BC) → ius gentium.
  • Constitutio Antoniniana (AD 212): citizenship to all free inhabitants → the ius civile/ius gentium distinction fades.

Ius civile / gentium / honorarium

  • Ius civile: law of Roman citizens (principle of personality of law).
  • Ius gentium: institutions common to all subjects of Rome (broadens the scope).
  • Ius honorarium: law created by magistrates when applying the law (progressive, flexible).

Corpus Juris Civilis (Justinian, 534)

Codex (imperial legislation) · Digest (the largest part: jurists' extracts) · Institutions (student textbook).

Ius privatum = three branches: law of persons (status: liberty/slavery, citizenship, family) · law of things (property, contract, delict, succession) · law of actions (remedies and procedure). Roman private law grew out of the law of actions.

🔑 Exam essentials3 pillars · Twelve Tables 450 BC · ius civile/gentium/honorarium · Corpus Juris Civilis (Codex/Digest/Institutions) · ius privatum (persons/things/actions).

10 Civil Law Tradition II — Ius Commune & Codification

  • 476 AD: fall of the Western Roman Empire → legal fragmentation; Roman law survives in vulgarised form, mixed with Germanic custom.
  • Revival (11th–12th c.): rediscovery of Justinian's Digest; the Empire vs Papacy struggle (both seeking legal justification); birth of the universities (Bologna, Paris, Salamanca) → professional jurists.
  • Ius commune = Roman + Canon + Germanic custom, a universalising factor of Continental Europe (uneven reception by region).
  • Humanists (historical-philological reading) + the School of Natural Law (Grotius, Pufendorf, Thomasius, Wolff) → ideological basis for codification.

The codification movement (nationalism + rationalism)

  • Early codes: Denmark (1683), Sweden (1734), Bavaria, Prussia (1794), Austria (1811).
  • French Civil Code 1804 (Code Napoléon): unifies French law; 3 pillars = private property, freedom of contract, the patriarchal family; rejects feudalism; flexible general rules (room for the judge).
  • German Civil Code / BGB 1900: 5 books (general, obligations, property, family, succession); conceptualism (precise definitions, 19th-c. German legal science); driven by unifying nationalism.
🔑 Exam essentials476 → revival (Digest/Bologna) · ius commune = Roman+Canon+Germanic · School of Natural Law · French Code 1804 (3 pillars) · BGB 1900 (5 books, conceptualism).
⚠️ Common trapsThe BGB (1900) is more modern than the French Code (1804): it recognises social obligations and gives women more power over property.

11 Civil Law — Defining Features Already examined — low priority

Analysed through 5 factors: history, mode of legal thinking, institutions, sources, ideology (these are generalisations).

  • Deductive reasoning: from general principles to concrete cases; a closed system (answers come from interpreting existing norms); law is "found, not made." French Civil Code Art. 4: a judge who refuses to decide on the pretext of silence/obscurity commits a denial of justice.
  • Institutions: private / public (administrative) / criminal courts; private law dominates development.
  • Hierarchy of sources: statutes > regulations > custom (only these three). No formal stare decisis, yet judges follow higher-court precedents in practice → jurisprudence constante.
  • Transformation: decodification (micro-systems, e.g. labour law); judge-made law (e.g. tort under French codes); rise of constitutionalism (German, Austrian, Italian, Spanish constitutional courts, French Conseil constitutionnel); impact of EU law and the ECHR.
🔑 Exam essentialsdeductive / closed system / found-not-made · French CC Art. 4 · sources statutes>regulations>custom · no stare decisis but jurisprudence constante · decodification + constitutionalism + EU law.
⚠️ Common trapsCivil law: the judge does not create law (in theory) and is not bound by formal precedent — the reverse of common law.

12 Common Law Tradition I — History

Bentham: the common law is "a thing merely imaginary." Austin: "childish fiction… common law is made by nobody" (a critique of the declaratory theory — that judges only "declare" pre-existing law).
  • 3 perspectives: a family of systems derived from English law; a system developed in England from the 12th c.; court-developed / case-based law (≠ statute law).
  • Global reach: former colonies (Australia, Canada except Quebec, New Zealand); partial (USA, Ireland, India, Pakistan, Malaysia, Singapore, Hong Kong); African hybrids.
  • Origins: pre-Norman = Germanic folk-law; Norman Conquest (1066) → centralisation; Henry II (1154–89) divides England into circuits and sends judges → law common to the whole country.
  • Writ system: an action begins with a writ from the chancery; each writ becomes a precedent; from the 14th c. writs issue only if facts match prior cases (Register of Writs); no suitable writ → no remedy. The forms of action classified and ossified actions → rigidity → injustice → hence equity arose.
  • 16th-c. crisis (pressure to receive Roman law) and 4 survival factors: (1) the Tudors preferred reshaping institutions; (2) new courts (Chancery, Star Chamber) covered deficiencies; (3) Coke restated/modernised its principles; (4) the Inns of Court preserved "the toughness of a taught tradition" (Maitland).
🔑 Exam essentialsBentham/Austin (declaratory theory) · Henry II circuits · writ system / forms of action / Register of Writs · 16th-c. crisis + 4 factors (Tudors, courts, Coke, Inns of Court).

13 The Rise of Equity

After the Norman Conquest (1066) the common law developed from the curia regis.

Why equity arose — shortcomings

  1. Limited writs (no suitable writ → no remedy).
  2. Inflexibility (rigidity → unconscionable results).
  3. Corruption and intimidation (courts swayed by the powerful).

→ Equity = fairness/justice; it fills the gap "whenever the common law might fall short of the ideal." It works alongside the common law.

Birth & systematisation

  • The wronged party petitions the monarch ("fountain of justice"), appealing to conscience → the King refers it to the Lord Chancellor → Court of Chancery (late 14th c.).
  • Systematisation: Lord Nottingham (1675) (principles), Lord Hardwicke (1737) (certainty/flexibility), Lord Eldon (1801) (precedent).

Conflict & resolution

  • Equity used the common injunction. Resolved under James I (Earl of Oxford's Case, 1615): equity prevails.
  • Judicature Acts 1873–75: (1) a single Supreme Court; (2) three Divisions; (3) any may apply common law and equity; (4) in conflict, equity shall prevail.
  • Merger debate: administration was fused; the systems? Ashburner: "the two streams… run side by side and do not mingle their waters" (NOT fused — the dominant view).

Maxims of Equity: He who seeks equity must do equity · Delay defeats equity · Where the equities are equal, the first in time prevails · Equity will not suffer a wrong to be without a remedy · Equity follows the law · Equity will not decree a vain thing · He who comes to equity must come with clean hands · Equity looks to the intent rather than the form. Equitable remedies: injunction, specific performance, rescission, rectification, account.

🔑 Exam essentials3 shortcomings · Chancellor/Chancery/conscience · Judicature Acts (equity prevails) · merger debate (Ashburner) · maxims · clean hands.

14 Common Law — Defining Features Already examined — low priority

5 factors: history, modes of thinking, institutions, sources, ideology.

  1. History: minor Roman-law influence; 1066 → feudalism; writ system; rise of equity.
  2. Empirical / case-based thinking: "think in pictures", reason from cases (≠ civil-law deductive). Ratio decidendi (binding) ≠ obiter dicta; found through the material facts (Lupton v FA & AB Ltd). Reasoning by analogy — Donoghue v Stevenson (the specific snail or "defective products"?). Pragmatism over logic.
  3. Institutions: haphazard development; procedure before substance; blurred public/private; organised by liability/remedies.
  4. Open system: evolves via judges; torts, trusts, estoppel; judicial creativity.
  5. Sources & ideology: Law & Equity (debt/damages vs injunctions/specific performance); statutory interpretation and the "myth of case-law dominance" (today heavily statutory; Parliamentary supremacy); ideology: private property, freedom of contract, feudal vestiges (fee simple, estates in land).
🔑 Exam essentialsratio decidendi vs obiter dicta · Donoghue v Stevenson · empiricism / think in pictures · pragmatism · myth of case-law dominance.
⚠️ Common trapsDon't confuse ratio (binding) with obiter (said in passing).

15 Sovereignty: from Westphalia to Globalization ★ Most likely

Peace of Westphalia (1648)

  • Pre-Westphalia: Ius Commune. The treaties divide Europe into sovereign Nation-States with exclusive power over their territory → law becomes national law.
  • Westphalian Duo: National State Law + International Public Law (the only two forms law could take).

Elements of the State

Sovereignty · Government · Territory & Population. Civil law → codes (Justinian, Napoleon); common law → Magna Carta (1215).

Classical theories of sovereignty

AuthorYearThesis
Jean Bodin16th c."absolute and perpetual power"; strong monarchy.
Thomas Hobbes1651Absolute sovereignty via the social contract (liberty for security).
John Locke1689Limited sovereignty; consent of the people.
Rousseau1762Popular sovereignty; the general will.

Types & challenges

  • Internal vs External · Legal vs Political · Negative vs Positive.
  • Challenges: globalisation, human rights, supranational institutions (EU, WTO, UN). From natural law to legal positivism (Westphalia → formal systems). Milestones: Magna Carta 1215 → Bill of Rights 1689 → US Constitution 1787 → French Revolution 1789.
🔑 Exam essentialsWestphalia 1648 + Westphalian duo · Bodin/Hobbes/Locke/Rousseau · types of sovereignty · 4 constitutional milestones.

16 Constitutionalism & Sovereignty Already examined — low priority

Constitutionalism = legal frameworks should limit government power.

Constitution (Finer): codes regulating the allocation of powers among government organs and their relationship with the public. It divides power between executive / legislative / judiciary — a counterweight to sovereignty.

Key characteristics

  1. Prior to government.
  2. Government created subject to it (constituent power; in a democracy, the people's).
  3. Superior to ordinary law (it empowers and limits).
  4. Entrenched (rigid, long-lived).

Forms & theory

  • Written/codified (US, Spain) vs Unwritten (UK).
  • Formal constitutionalism: the constitution = norms with absolute legal supremacy (apex; judicial enforcement, central or diffuse; constitutional review). From this view, neither the UK nor China have "real" constitutions.
  • Material constitutionalism: only certain constitutions are "real" → links the constitution to (representative) democracy and limited government: Democratic ("government of the people, by the people, for the people") and Liberal (separation of powers + (human) rights, "government of laws, not of men"). Those lacking these = "façade" constitutions.
🔑 Exam essentials4 characteristics · constituent power · written vs unwritten · formal vs material · democratic vs liberal · façade constitutions.

17 Constitutionalism & Fundamental Rights Already examined — low priority

Protecting rights = a central task of 20th-c. constitutions; a check on internal sovereignty. "Without fundamental rights there is no constitution."

Origins

  • Natural Law (Maritain): human dignity → the person is a subject of rights.
  • American Declaration of Independence: "endowed by their Creator with certain unalienable Rights: Life, Liberty and the pursuit of Happiness."
  • Positive Law (Bentham): "Natural rights is simple nonsense."

Concepts

Formal (in the constitution / specially protected) · Procedural (distrust of the democratic process: not left to simple majorities) · Substantial (an ideal dimension: they positivise human rights as moral rights).

Three generations

Gen.ContentType
1st — Civil & PoliticalLiberal origin (Locke).Negative rights (State non-intervention).
2nd — Economic, Social & CulturalHousing, health, education… "Human rights begin with breakfast" (Senghor); welfare state.Positive rights (State action).
3rd — CollectiveSelf-determination, environment, resources.Collective, controversial.

2nd-gen controversy: implementation, intrusive judicial enforcement, budgetary impact, separation of powers. Enforcement: Thin (review only of the executive) vs Thick (also parliamentary legislation).

🔑 Exam essentialsnatural vs positive law (Maritain/Bentham) · formal/procedural/substantial · 3 generations (negative vs positive) · thin vs thick.

18 International Law — Nature / Sources / Hierarchy ★ Most likely

18.1 Nature

  • Higgins: the law governing relations between States and with international organisations; "our common language."
  • Three elements: Public · International · Law (but consent-based).
  • No centralised institutions: no parliament (→ ILC, UN GA), no unified court system (ICJ, ICC…), no global police (UN peacekeeping). Henkin: "Almost all nations observe almost all principles of international law… almost all of the time."
  • Principles: sovereign equality · respect for independence · prohibition on war (UN Charter Art. 2(3)-(4); exception Art. 51 self-defence).
  • Critiques (Global South, Rajagopal); what is it for? (power / emancipation / form / justice).

18.2 Sources

  • Formal (create law) vs Material (evidence it, e.g. custom).
  • Article 38 ICJ Statute: (1) conventions/treaties; (2) custom; (3) general principles; (4) subsidiary means (judicial decisions + doctrine).
  • Custom = state practice + opinio juris. Persistent objector (not bound if it objects from the start); new states are bound.
  • Treaties (VCLT Art. 2(1)): a written agreement between States; bind only those who consent. A treaty can bind / develop / crystallise / codify custom.
  • Subsidiary: no binding precedent (Art. 59 ICJ Statute); soft law (GA resolutions, ILC).

18.3 Hierarchy

  • Relative normativity: no formal hierarchy of sources; content matters.
  • Jus cogens: peremptory norms (VCLT Arts. 53 & 64 → a conflicting treaty is void); accepted by the community "as a whole." List: slavery & apartheid, crimes against humanity, war crimes, genocide, torture. Case-law: Nicaragua (1986) → Furundžija (1998) → Congo v Rwanda (2006) → Belgium v Senegal (2012).
  • Erga omnes: obligations owed to the whole community; any State may invoke responsibility. All jus cogens is erga omnes, not vice versa.
  • Conflicts: lex specialis · lex posterior; fragmentation.
🔑 Exam essentialsArticle 38 · custom = practice + opinio juris · persistent objector · 4 treaty↔custom routes · jus cogens (VCLT 53/64 + list + cases) · erga omnes · lex specialis/posterior.

19 The European Union I — Understanding the EU ★ Most likely

The EU pools the sovereignty of its States: neither a federation (like the US) nor a mere IO (like the UN).

Legal foundation

  • Treaty-based · Rule of law (the ECJ ensures compliance) · Direct effect (creates rights for individuals) · Primacy (EU law prevails over national law).
  • Constitutional qualities: individual rights, institutional balance, judicial review, conferred powers.

History

  • 1951 ECSC (Coal & Steel) → 1957 Treaties of Rome / EEC → enlargements (UK/Ireland/Denmark 1973; Spain & Portugal 1986) → Maastricht 1993 → the EU is born → Amsterdam/Nice → Lisbon 2009 (after the Constitutional Treaty was rejected).
  • 3 pillars (1993–2009): (1) EC — supranational, internal market, monetary union; (2) CFSP — intergovernmental; (3) JHA (Justice & Home Affairs) — intergovernmental.

Institutions

European Parliament (elected, co-legislates) · Council of the EU (ministers) · European Commission (proposes & implements) · Court of Justice (CJEU) (uniform interpretation).

Landmark cases

  • Van Gend en Loos (1963): creates the doctrine of direct effect (EU law gives individuals rights national courts must protect).
  • Costa v ENEL (1964): establishes the primacy of EU law over national law ("an independent source of law").

20 The European Union II — Scope & Intensity of EU Law ★ Most likely

The EU acts only on conferred powers (unlike a sovereign State).

Three principles

  1. Enumeration / Conferral: every action needs a legal basis in the Treaties; what is not conferred stays with the States.
  2. Subsidiarity: "should the EU act even though it can?" — only if the States cannot achieve the objective sufficiently. Check: "yellow cards" from national parliaments.
  3. Proportionality: action must not exceed what is necessary.

Competences

ExclusiveOnly the EU (customs union, competition rules, common commercial policy, marine resources).
SharedEU and States (in conflict, the EU prevails by supremacy).
Complementary / SupportingThe EU supports/coordinates.

Teleological interpretation: the ECJ prefers reading by the telos/purpose (over historical or literal) → maximum discretion → an expansive view of competences.

21 Islamic Law — The Shariah

  • Shariah = rules revealed by God; its validity is the will of Allah, not dependent on any earthly legislator.
  • Key difference: the West changes law to fit society; Islam holds that society must adapt to the law. Jurists do not create, they discover. It regulates all of life.
  • Four roots (codified by As-Shafi'i, d. 820): Qur'an (supreme, few legal verses) → Sunna (the Prophet's practice; "largely unhistorical", Schacht) → Ijma (consensus; the critical role) → Qiyas (analogy).
  • Four schools: Maliki (Medina; N/W/Central Africa) · Hanafi (Kufa, Iraq; Near/Middle East, Indus) · Shafi'i (E. Africa, Malaysia, Indonesia) · Hanbali (Saudi Arabia).
  • Rigidity & hiyal: evasion devices (the double sale to dodge usury/riba); the Shariah was confined to private law.
  • Modernisation (from 1850): the Majalla (1869–76); Turkey → Swiss Civil Code (1926) (a radical break); Anglo-Muhammadan Law (British India, Common Law principles → case-law).
  • Reislamification: the Iranian Revolution (Khomeini) reversed secularisation (Iran: Supreme Supervisory Council).
🔑 Exam essentialsdivine foundation (society adapts to law) · 4 roots (Qur'an/Sunna/Ijma/Qiyas) · 4 schools · hiyal/double sale · Turkey 1926 · reislamification (Iran).

22 Law in the Far East

  • Fundamental difference: the West regulates with objective rules + courts; the Far East prefers conciliation, compromise, arbitration over litigation.
  • No "Far Eastern legal family": China and Japan are fundamentally different (shared Confucian heritage).
  • Confucianism & 'li': a universe in harmony; the rules of li are calibrated by status; whoever litigates is seen as a disruptive, uncultivated person; virtue = forbearance.
  • Legists vs Confucians: Legists (man is selfish; severe penal sanctions; all equal before the law) vs Confucians (man is good; education; respect for status). The TS'IN (221 BC) favoured the Legists (burning books); the HAN (206 BC) restored Confucianism as state ideology for 2,000 years.
  • Chinese codes: mostly criminal & administrative (penalties graded by status); private law almost absent (resolved outside the courts).
  • Modern China: unequal treaties (1850) → 1911 republic (Kuomintang: German/Swiss law) → 1949 Mao → Cultural Revolution (legal collapse, lawyers persecuted) → Deng (Fundamentals of Private Law, 1987); open future (Lubman: without political reform, law won't have the value the West attributes).
  • Japan: Tokugawa (1603–1867) feudal/Confucian → Meiji: 1853 US ships → 1889 Prussian-style constitution → 1898 Civil Code based on the German BGB → after WWII the 1946 American-influenced constitution. Living law: conciliation persists ("invisible accounts"); debate (Haley/Oda: overburdened courts, not only Confucius).
🔑 Exam essentialsconciliation > litigation · no single family · li · Legists vs Confucians (TS'IN/HAN) · China: 1949 / Cultural Revolution / Deng 1987 · Japan: BGB 1898 + American influence post-1945 · "reception ≠ implementation".

23 Glossary & Key Cases

Express glossary

  • Rule of law — no one is above the law; general, clear, prospective, impartial rules.
  • Validity / Efficacy / Justice — belonging to the system (binding) / actual compliance / fit with values.
  • Efficacy vs Efficiency — is it obeyed? vs did it achieve its goal?
  • Is/ought gap (Hume) — you cannot derive the "ought" from the "is".
  • Social thesis / Separability thesis — law = social fact / no necessary law–morality link.
  • ILP vs ELP — morality can / can never be a validity criterion.
  • Law as prophecy (Holmes) — law = prediction of what courts will do.
  • Veil of ignorance (Rawls) / Maximin — design without knowing your position / maximise the minimum.
  • Entitlement theory (Nozick) — a distribution is just if reached by voluntary exchange.
  • Twelve Tables — first Roman codification (450 BC). Corpus Juris Civilis — Codex/Digest/Institutions (Justinian, 534).
  • Ius civile / gentium / honorarium — law of citizens / common to all / created by magistrates.
  • Ius commune — Roman + Canon + Germanic custom.
  • Writ / Forms of action — formal order enabling an action / rigid categories of actions.
  • Ratio decidendi / Obiter dicta — binding reason / non-binding remark.
  • Stare decisis / Jurisprudence constante — binding precedent (common) / persuasive trend (civil).
  • Direct effect / Primacy — rights for individuals (Van Gend) / EU supremacy (Costa).
  • Subsidiarity / Proportionality / Conferral — act only if States cannot / not beyond necessary / only conferred powers.
  • Regulation vs Directive — directly applicable vs requires transposition.
  • Jus cogens / Erga omnes — peremptory non-derogable norm / obligation owed to the whole community.
  • Opinio juris — belief in legal obligation (custom). Persistent objector — State not bound by objecting from the start.
  • Lex iniusta non est lex — an unjust law is not law (Aquinas). Hiyal — Islamic legal evasion. Li — Confucian rules of conduct.
